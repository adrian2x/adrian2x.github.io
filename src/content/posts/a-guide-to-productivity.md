Productivity has been talked about endlessly. It seems every day there's a new app or a new book or a new something out there that will change your life and revolutionize your productivity to take it to the next level.

But will it, really? Or will it give you yet another thing to do before you realize it's not for you? How much will you pay for that course or that coach or that service? How much will that money be worth once you realize the only system that works is the one that works for _you_?

The only person who can make it work is you, by building a system designed for your specific needs. So let's build that system. All you need to start is a good list. Three of them, actually.

### A to-do list

This is where you keep track of the things you **must do** - commitments, assignments, anything that needs doing. You can take it further and categorize by due date (this week, next week, next month). Everything that must be done goes here.

### A watch list

The watch list is everything you're waiting on: things to remember, or to follow up on once someone else gets back to you.

### A later list

Everything else goes on a later list - things you'll do when you have the time, or wish you could do someday.

---

If something doesn't fit on any of these lists, it probably isn't that important, since you won't even want to do it later. So just ignore it.

Every morning, look at your to-do list and pick 3 to 5 things to get done that day. Then try as hard as you can to actually do them. Get them done and it's a good day, and you feel accomplished.

Once this becomes a habit, you notice how many days used to go by without a single important thing getting done. Every time you finish one, you cross it off the list and get that little hit of dopamine that makes you feel proud and productive.

One nice thing about this list: if you catch yourself procrastinating on a particular to-do, you can pick something else on the list to do in the meantime.

As [John Perry](http://www.structuredprocrastination.com/), a philosophy professor from Stanford, would put it:

> The list of tasks one has in mind will be ordered by importance. Tasks that seem most urgent and important are on top. But there are also worthwhile tasks to perform lower down on the list. Doing these tasks becomes a way of not doing the things higher up on the list. With this sort of appropriate task structure, the procrastinator becomes a useful citizen. Indeed, the procrastinator can even acquire, as I have, a reputation for getting a lot done.

### Only check your email twice a day

Checking email only twice a day frees up the rest of it. You're distracted less often and have more time to focus on the task in front of you. Anyone who absolutely cannot wait until the end of the day will call, text, or reach you some other way. Email kills your flow and takes away your ability to focus on one thing long enough to be productive.

You can take this further by sorting your email with labels and filters, so messages land in an _Action_, _Pending_, or _Review_ folder. Everything else you archive as soon as the conversation is done.

### Turn off your notifications

Same as with email, being constantly pinged about your friend's latest adventures or meals is distracting and interrupts your flow. Turn off the notifications and check your phone when you have time.

### Don't answer the phone

Unless you're waiting for an important call, put your phone on silent and let calls go to voicemail. If it's important, they'll leave a message. When you have time, go through your voicemail and return the important calls.

Don't answer the phone from random numbers, either. There are a lot of robo-callers, spam marketers, and even scammers out there. Don't fall for it.

I only ever answer calls from known numbers in my contacts. That's it.

### Start the day right

No matter what you do or what time you wake up, start the day right. That might mean a nice breakfast, a light workout, or some meditation. Whatever suits you, it helps to have a moment to peacefully collect your thoughts and prepare mentally and emotionally for the day ahead.

It's also a chance to review your to-do list and gather anything you'll need for the first task.

That's the whole system. It isn't much, which is sort of the point.

---

PS. One last thought. Next time you're about to put something on your to-do list, consider whether you want to do it at all. Does it give you a sense of purpose? Life is too short for endless to-do lists; it's meant to be lived and appreciated. Don't get fooled by productivity for its own sake. It's all about getting the **important** stuff done and freeing up time for the things that make your life **better**.
